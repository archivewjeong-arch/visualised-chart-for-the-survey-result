1

Description

What happened?

The submission deadline for Assignment 1, which covered the content of EDUC5011 Modules 1 through 3, was May 25. This assignment required creating the first three pages of a Google Sites e-Portfolio to demonstrate my digital literacy knowledge and professional learning goals. About a week later, I received feedback via Turnitin.

In the Week 1 survey, I rated my digital literacy confidence as moderate. While my information retrieval and evaluation skills were great, I felt a lack of knowledge regarding tool selection and designing collaborative assignments for students.
2

Feelings

How did I feel?

I had lost a lot of motivation when I started A1. This was because I felt that the tutorial conducted at the beginning of the class was uninteresting and hindered my immersion. Due to a lack of meaningful feedback and active student participation, I lost interest in the course material and decided that I just needed to pass. Because of this mindset, I also lacked preparation time.

However, after meeting with Tim, I realised that this course was worth studying seriously, and I began to immerse myself in the assessment with a different mindset. But by then, time was already running out.
3

Evaluation

What went well and what did not?

Criterion 1 (D)
I clearly identified my strengths and weaknesses, however, not explained deeply enough. I could have articulated deeper, for example, what do they look like in your specific teaching context? How do they connect to my professional situation?

Criteria 2, 3, 5 (HD)
The feedback confirmed that my understanding of pedagogy, academic writing, and curriculum alignment was correct. I purposefully chose the academic literature after in-depth analysis and critical evaluation to connect with my teaching area and Digital literacy General capability, and it was recognised by the marker and unit assessor, which was both motivating and encouraging.

Criterion 4 (C)
Overall, the look and flow of my website was satisfying but insufficient use of hyperlinks connecting to external texts beyond my site and could have been much more interactive if I had used a wider range of digital tools instead of presenting text-heavy sections. This is the part I should focus on in A2. And the introduction video lacked visual richness, which I neglected.

I had prioritised content quality over presentation creativity, partly due to time constraints, and a lack of full understanding of the meaning of 'interactive' in this context.

4

Analysis

Why did this happen?

Falloon's (2020) TDC framework clearly explains the room for improvement. I knew what interactivity meant in theory, but I did not know how to express it in a digital environment. I think I was so used to academic writing that I had not yet developed a clear sense of what engagement looks like on a public-facing website.

I had CK and PK, but my TK, specifically understanding the role and potential of digital tools for professional communication, had not caught up (Mishra, 2019). Contextual knowledge matters. The academic writing context and the digital presentation context are different environments, and I had not yet made that translation.

Looking at the Digital Literacy General Capability (ACARA, 2022b), I can see that I was operating confidently in Investigating and Creating and Exchanging, but I had not yet applied Managing and Operating to my own professional work. Blannin's (2022) RRR framework explains the gap simply: I reflected and reviewed, but I skipped the reconsider step when it came to how I was actually presenting my ideas.

5

Conclusion

What did I learn?

My biggest takeaway is that engagement precedes quality. If I had approached this topic with genuine curiosity from Week 1, the depth and interactivity of my work would have been stronger.

I also learned that how I present and communicate my own professional thinking is just as central to digital literacy as what I teach, which is something Falloon's (2020) TDC framework made clear to me. I had only thought about what I teach students before.

A Google Site has become a professional outcome that shows my identity as a digitally competent educator.

6

Action Plan

What will I do next?

For Assessment 2, I have deliberately chosen tools that lighten text-heavy presentations such as (1) Genially: interactive government maps, (2) Thinglink: annotated ballot papers, (3) Canva: visual civic newsletters, (4) Padlet: collegial collaboration and peer feedback.

Each tool was selected not only for its pedagogical value but also to address the interactivity gap Catherine identified in my A1 feedback.

Going forward, I will apply the RRR framework (Blannin, 2022) to my own professional presentation choices, not just my lesson planning, asking myself at every stage whether there is a more engaging, more interactive way to communicate what I know.
